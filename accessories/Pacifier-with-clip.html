How pacifier prevent sids?

Pacifiers are no longer just for soothing colicky babies. According to a new study, using a pacifier while sleeping reduces the chances of a baby dying from sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS) by 90%. Pacifiers also eliminated the increased risk associated with babies sleeping on their stomachs or in soft bedding, both of which have been shown to increase the risk of SIDS by up to tenfold. "A baby who sleeps on his stomach without a pacifier has a 2.5 times greater risk of SIDS," says De-Kun Li, the study's lead author and a reproductive epidemiologist at Kaiser Permanente in Oakland, California. "By using a pacifier, you eliminate that baby's risk."

What are natural latex pacifiers?

Natural rubber latex is a natural material derived from the Hevea tree's sticky milky fluid. The fluid is refined to the brown/yellow rubber you're familiar with from pacifiers; a super soft, elastic, and resilient material that closely resembles the mother's soft nipple.

The natural material ages naturally as a result of natural influences such as UV light, air, saliva, and heat. Because of the elasticity of the material, the nipple can change shape and size due to the baby's strong vacuum, which means that the nipple can expand in some cases if it is not replaced in a timely manner.

Pacifier latex vs Silicone. What should you choose?

Natural rubber latex is a natural substance, whereas silicone is an industrial substance. We use food-grade silicone in our pacifiers, which is free of harmful and endocrine-disrupting ingredients like BPA, PVC, and phthalates.

Durability: Natural rubber latex ages naturally as a result of natural influences such as UV light, air, saliva, and heat. Silicone pacifiers are more durable than latex pacifiers because they keep their shape, do not age, can withstand high temperatures, and last longer. They are not, however, as soft as latex pacifiers.

Replacement: We recommend replacing our pacifiers every 4-6 weeks for safety and hygiene reasons, regardless of material.

Natural rubber latex is soft and resembles a mother's breast more than silicone. Most people start with natural rubber latex because it has a more natural feel and softness, but it all comes down to what your baby prefers.

Allergy: Silicone is an allergy-friendly material, so if someone in your family has a latex allergy, you should use silicone.

Smell and taste: Silicone is odorless and tasteless, whereas natural rubber latex can have a slight rubber odor and taste.

Natural rubber latex should be scalded rather than boiled when cleaning! Fill the pacifiers with boiling water and soak them for about 5 minutes. There is no microwave sterilization. Silicone pacifiers should also be soaked in boiling water for approximately 5 minutes. Silicone can be sterilized in the microwave.

Babies are unique. There is no right or wrong material for a pacifier's nipple because every baby is unique. It may be necessary to experiment with various materials in order to find the best fit for your child. Some babies prefer one material over another, while others have no preferences and will accept whatever you offer.

Why pacifier is important?

Physicians are frequently asked for advice on pacifier use in children, particularly the benefits and risks, as well as when to wean a child. Pacifier use has analgesic effects, shortens hospital stays for preterm infants, and lowers the risk of sudden infant death syndrome. Pacifiers have been studied and recommended for pain relief in newborns and infants undergoing minor procedures in the emergency room (e.g., heel sticks, immunizations, venipuncture). To reduce the risk of sudden infant death syndrome, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that parents offer pacifiers to infants one month and older at the onset of sleep.

Potential pacifier complications include a negative effect on breastfeeding, dental malocclusion, and otitis media. Adverse dental effects can be seen as early as two years of age, but are most noticeable after four years. To avoid breastfeeding difficulties, the American Academy of Family Physicians recommends that mothers be educated about pacifier use in the immediate postpartum period. To prevent otitis media, the American Academy of Pediatrics and the American Academy of Family Physicians recommend weaning children from pacifiers in the second six months of life. Pacifier use should not be discouraged and may be especially beneficial during the first six months of life.
